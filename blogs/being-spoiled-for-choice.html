Being Spoiled for Choice

2 August 2023

During the academic year, I read or heard about the problem people have with doing something (yes, I’ve forgotten entirely what that "something" is or was, but bear with me). This issue most people had with this something was never being able to get a concrete answer when it came to finding a good resource when learning something new.

With that covered, I can now apply it to linear algebra and actually make sense.

Linear algebra was one of the hardest but also one of the coolest courses I had the privilege of taking. From vector spaces to eigenvalues, MAT185 opened my eyes to just how abstract but applicable the subject is. There is not a day where I would face problems where I'm told to solve the components of a circuit or even prove the operation of a derivative and I would consult the tools I learned in linear algebra to overcome it. However one of the biggest issues I came across was right as the course started, and it was about not knowing where to start. I was scratching my head on whether or not I should review the prerequisite course’ notes or if I should consult another textbook as a supplement. Figuring out where to even start is always difficult. It always feels like a commitment - the first few notes you take must stay there and the first principles you learn are indicative of the final product. And when this bit fails, it can dishearten you and cause you to struggle when you try to take it anywhere further.

Many universities teach a first course in linear algebra that focuses on computation and is rather uncommitted, followed by a second course which focuses more on the abstract and structure theory. In my opinion, this is because of the inertia of how much content can be covered and departments having to teach service linear algebra while not offering a separate math major in conjunction. Really, it's better to see both approaches in tandem, so I skipped pass books like Strang and I do comment when books calling themselves "second courses" only do so because they internalize the dichotomy, rather than explicitly assuming a student’s background. Many books also restrict focus to rank and column or even characteristics from the get go. This saddens me since (for example) finite fields are cool, but that may resonate with very little.

So here is my advice: master first principles. Once you’ve gotten a solid foundation (or ‘gimmick’, although that springs up the wrong images), you can work outwards. How can you exploit coverage in other books to maximize your learning? How does that single idea, that single principle, open up the door to an entire experience? You know you’ve struck gold and hit a good idea when the floodgates open in your head and it grows from there.

Always work from the inside out. Forget about choosing a textbook. Instead, think of the connections between concepts. The simpler the better, and once you’ve got this into your head, everything becomes a lot lot easier.
