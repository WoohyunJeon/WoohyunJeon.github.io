UT Atlas

Atlas is a fixed-wing drone with a VTOL configuration, designed to carry a passenger payload for the 2024 AEAC competition. The design prioritized aerodynamic efficiency and operational radius of 10 km. However, challenges in balancing the mass budget and acceptable levels of torsional stiffness led to the project being discontinued before it was entered into the initial design proposal. In its place, UT Cyclone II was redesigned for the competition.

I was directly involved in manufacturing the control surfaces using carbon fiber layups and conducted CFD and FEA in Ansys Fluent and SolidWorks to design, analyze, and optimize the design.

UT Cyclone II

Cyclone II is a hexacopter drone with a spiral configuration, designed to compete in place of UT Atlas for the 2024 AEAC competition. The design was able to carry a 2 kg passenger payload over 13 km and the total mass weighed in at 14 kg. With Cyclone II, the team scored 1st in the on-site presentation.

I was in charge of researching and developing the team's proprietary prepreg carbon fiber, with a focus on ensuring spare parts were available to address potential catastrophic failures. My efforts enabled the team to perform post-flight repairs, including restoring the drone's arms and landing gear, ensuring Cyclone II was airworthy for the next competition phase.

PID Tuning Stand

The PID tuning stand is a single-axis stabilization platform designed to enable the team to test and fine-tune the PID parameters of copter-drone designs.

I was tasked with researching the feasibility of designing a gyroscopic stabilizer for tuning the drones' PID parameters. However, I proposed to design a single-axis stand to simplify the process, leveraging the symmetrical nature of most drones.

By designing the tuning stand, the team significantly reduces the budget required for spare parts by eliminating the risk of catastrophic failures caused by crash landings.
